Weaving Words, Bridging Worlds

I am a Nigerian writer, poet, blogger, podcaster, and entrepreneur based in Abuja. With roots in both creative literature and academic research, I bridge the worlds of artistic expression and scholarly analysis.

About Me

My journey as a storyteller began long before I put pen to paper. Growing up in Northern Nigeria, I was surrounded by rich oral traditions—folktales told under moonlight, proverbs that carried generations of wisdom, and poetry that danced between Hausa, Arabic, and English.

These early influences shaped my desire to capture the human experience in all its complexity. My work explores themes of faith, identity, love, and the delicate threads that connect us across cultures.

Today, I write to bridge worlds—between tradition and modernity, between Nigeria and the global stage, between the spiritual and the everyday. Every word I craft is an invitation to see the world through new eyes.

My Story

In my early years, at primary school, in the 1980s, I stumbled upon an amazing poem about scars. Our teacher, bless her heart, explained how scars aren't just physical marks but reminders of the battles we've fought. That lesson stuck with me, inspiring me to write two poetry collections and four novels since then.

Despite my natural shyness and reluctance to participate in public events like poetry readings and competitions, I've found joy in sharing my work online, thanks to the wonders of technology. My academic journey includes earning a BSc in International Studies from A.B.U Zaria and an MSc in International Relations and Diplomacy from Nile University, Abuja — completing my thesis in 2024 on COVID-19 vaccine nationalism and dependency in Africa.

Today, I'm happily settled in Abuja, Nigeria, with my husband and two children, embracing the balance between family life and my passion for writing and learning.

A Taste of Nigeria

Stories from my kitchen — where flavor meets memory, and every dish carries a piece of home.

🍗 Nigerian Peppered Chicken

Deeply spiced, crispy fried chicken tossed in a rich, fragrant pepper sauce.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 25 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8 🌶️ Heat: Bold
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 1 whole chicken (cut into pieces)
  • 1 large or 2 medium bell peppers
  • 4 attarugu (scotch bonnet) or hot peppers
  • 1 tomato, 1 large onion
  • Salt, curry powder, thyme, chicken seasoning
  • 1 double Knorr chicken cube and 1 Royco cube
  • Oil for frying
📖 View Method
  1. Boil the Chicken: Cut chicken into pieces, wash, and place in a pot with 1 tsp salt, ½ tsp curry, chicken seasoning, Royco cube, thyme, and half the chopped onion. Cover and cook for 30 mins until soft.
  2. Fry the Chicken: Remove chicken pieces and deep-fry in hot oil until golden brown. Set aside.
  3. Prepare the Pepper Sauce: Blend remaining onion, bell peppers, hot peppers, tomato, and garlic. Cook mixture until water dries up, then add oil and fry until oil separates.
  4. Combine and Finish: Season sauce with ½ tsp salt, curry, chicken seasoning, thyme, and 1 Knorr cube. Fry 30 seconds, then add fried chicken. Mix well and fry another 30 seconds.

✨ Serve hot with jollof rice, fried rice, boiled yam, or plantain.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: The 30+30 seconds rule keeps chicken crispy. Add smoked paprika for extra smokiness. Reduce scotch bonnets to 2 for milder heat.

🥣 Eba & Okro Soup

Smooth, golden eba paired with rich, slimy okra soup — a wholesome taste of home.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 55 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 500g beef (cut into chunks)
  • 2 cups okra (chopped)
  • 1 onion (chopped)
  • 2 scotch bonnet peppers
  • 1 bell pepper, 1 tomato
  • 1 tsp iru (locust beans)
  • 1 tsp liquid potash (akanwu)
  • Seasoning cubes, salt, curry, thyme
  • 2 cups garri (for eba)
📖 View Method

Okro Soup: Boil beef with onion, garlic, salt, stock cube, curry, thyme. Blend onion, bell pepper, tomatoes, scotch bonnet. Fry until oil separates. Add water, salt, stock cubes, iru. Boil 5 mins. Add chopped okra, cook 5 mins. Add liquid potash, cook 5 mins. Add beef and stock.

Eba: Boil 2 cups water. Slowly sprinkle garri while stirring until thick. Cover on low heat 5 mins. Stir vigorously, cover, rest 10 mins. Shape and serve.

✨ Serve warm, with the soup generously ladled over the eba.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Liquid potash (akanwu) gives okro soup its signature texture. For smoother eba, stir vigorously in one direction and let it rest covered.

🍲 Semo & Egusi Soup

A classic Nigerian comfort meal — rich melon seed stew with semolina.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 70 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 2 cups egusi (melon seeds, ground)
  • 500g beef or assorted meat
  • 1 cup smoked fish or stockfish
  • 2 cups pumpkin leaves (ugwu) or spinach
  • 1 onion, 2 scotch bonnet peppers
  • 1 bell pepper, 2 tomatoes
  • ½ cup palm oil
  • 2 tbsp crayfish (ground)
  • Seasoning cubes, salt
  • 2 cups semolina flour (for semo)
📖 View Method

Egusi Soup: Boil beef with salt and Maggi. Mix egusi with crayfish into paste. Fry garlic, onions in oil. Add blended peppers, fry until soft. Add beef, crayfish, daddawa, seasonings. Add fish. Scoop egusi paste, cook 7 mins, stir, cook 5 more mins. Add remaining palm oil, ugwu, water leaves, cook 5 mins.

Semolina: Boil water. Mix semo with cold water into paste. Pour into boiling water, cook 5 mins (careful — it rises!). Slowly add semolina while stirring until thick. Cook 5 mins, turn off heat, let finish on hot stove.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Add smoked fish for richer flavor. Adjust water for consistency. Some prefer egusi in lumps, others broken — both are delicious.

🍚 Nigerian Party Jollof Rice

The legendary one-pot party rice — smoky, vibrant, unforgettable.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 50 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 4 cups long-grain rice (rinsed)
  • 800g fresh tomato blend + 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 large onions (diced)
  • 3-4 scotch bonnet peppers
  • ½ cup vegetable oil
  • 2 tbsp thyme, 2 tbsp curry powder
  • 3 seasoning cubes & salt to taste
  • 4 cups chicken stock
  • 1 tsp garlic powder, 1 tsp ginger powder
  • 2 bay leaves
📖 View Method
  1. Blend tomatoes, scotch bonnet into smooth puree. Fry in hot oil until reduced and deep red.
  2. Add diced onions, garlic, ginger, fry 3 mins. Add tomato paste, curry, thyme, seasoning cubes.
  3. Pour in chicken stock and bring to a boil. Add bay leaves and rinsed rice.
  4. Cover and cook on low heat until rice absorbs liquid (~25-30 mins).

✨ For smoky flavor, let it burn slightly at bottom. Serve with fried plantain.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Add 1 tsp Nigerian curry powder and ½ tsp white pepper for deeper warmth. Leftover Jollof fried with onions makes 'Jollof rice stir-fry' — even better the next day!

🍚 Peten Chinkafa

The original Fulani comfort food — passed down through generations, now shared with the world.

By Hadiza Bagudu • First published online May 2026

⏱️ Prep: 25 mins 🍲 Cook: 60 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8 🌡️ Best: Cold days & mornings
💡 Hadiza's Note: This is my ultimate comfort food when I have the flu. The warmth of the peppers, the richness of the stock fish, and the sourness of yakuwa — it heals from the inside out.
🌿 View Ingredients
  • Short grain white rice
  • Beef
  • Bonga fish
  • Stock fish
  • Crayfish
  • Attarugu (scotch bonnet peppers)
  • Red bell pepper
  • Green bell pepper 🫑
  • Tomatoes 🍅
  • Green bitter garden eggs
  • Vegetable oil
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Garlic
  • Yakuwa (sour spinach)
  • Bitter leaf (optional)
  • Curry & thyme
  • Daddawa (locust beans)
  • Salt
  • Cabbage
📖 View Method
  1. Boil the meat: Boil beef with onions, garlic, curry and thyme until soft and containing stock. Set aside.
  2. Prepare the fish: Break and open the bonga and stock fish in a bowl (except crayfish), removing sharp bones. Cover with boiling water and leave for 10 minutes to clean. Discard the water, wash again with cold water, then add to the cooked meat with stock.
  3. Prepare the pepper base: Roughly blend or pound the peppers, attarugu, one onion, and one garlic. Cook until the water is gone, then fry until darker. Add to the meat pot.
  4. Add garden eggs: Chop garden eggs and add to the meat pot with about 2 liters of water. Add salt, seasonings, and daddawa. Allow to cook for a few minutes.
  5. Cook the rice: Wash rice well, removing stones, then add to the pot. Allow to cook until soft. Important: Make sure the water is more than the rice so when the rice is soft, it is still watery — like porridge.
  6. Finish with vegetables: At that stage, add chopped cabbage, garden eggs, onion, green pepper, and bitter leaf. Cook for 5 more minutes.

✨ That's it. Your delicious Fulani comfort food PETE is done. Enjoy. Best eaten on cold days and in the morning.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: The secret to authentic Peten Chinkafa is the water-to-rice ratio — it must finish watery, like porridge. The yakuwa (sour spinach) adds a distinctive tang that balances the richness of the fish. If you can't find yakuwa, substitute with sorrel leaves or add a squeeze of lime at the end.

A Tragedy and a Miracle

April 17, 2026 • 5 min read

Personal Faith

Oh, grief.

Something terrible happened to me on the 17th of April, this month, 2026.

I was in my room when my girl came in and told me that… something bad had happened. A terrible accident.

Somebody reversed a car… and killed my cats.

You see, a few weeks back, my female cat gave birth to four kittens. Four full, lovely kittens.

I loved them so much.

We raised them for weeks. They were still tiny, still suckling, even though they had started eating small food, and they were growing so well. Healthy. Beautiful.

We named them — Gimbia (Princess), Funke, Chioma, and Bodejo (Ginger). Four beautiful names, after the four famous tribes in Nigeria.

I even opened a social media page for them — The Agebutter Squad. It was supposed to be wholesome. I wanted to post their daily shenanigans, their little lives, everything.

Just that morning, I had tried to upload a video of them playing. It was too heavy to post, but I still wrote about them — about the food we cooked, how we tried to make it nutritious.

It was all so beautiful.

And then, suddenly… somebody crushed the heads of my cats.

It is such a bizarre incident. I still don't understand how it even happened with a car. Three of them died instantly.

The fourth kitten was missing.

One of them… I thought it was still alive. I rushed it to the veterinary hospital, hoping. But it was declared dead.

We couldn't find the last kitten.

I didn't even know how to react. I was just… heartbroken. In pain.

After a while, I tried to accept that they were gone. But I was grieving them more than I thought I was capable of.

The mother… she refused to eat. We put food for her, but she wouldn't touch it. She just kept calling out for her children.

Oh my God.

It is the most heartbreaking thing.

───

Four days passed.

And then… something happened.

Something I can only call a miracle.

Someone in my house found the last kitten somewhere in our neighbourhood, near our mosque.

That kitten had been surviving on its own.

People had been giving it food. And somehow… it was still alive. Not even injured.

Oh my God.

As soon as it came home, it went straight to the mother and started suckling. And the mother… she started eating again.

It was the most incredible thing.

Yes, I lost three kittens.

But this… this is still so amazing.

Only Allah knows why things happen the way they do.

Sometimes, truly horrible things happen in life. And Allah has already told us in the Qur'an — that we will be tested. With fear, with pain, with loss.

And in the past few years, I have been tested with death a lot. My father died. One of my favorite in-laws died. Others too.

So I have been grieving… a lot.

And then this, on top of everything — these kittens.

It hurt so deeply.

But I accept it as a test from Allah. I pray that I passed.

And I pray that, by His mercy, I will be reunited with them again.

One thing that breaks my heart now… is the surviving kitten.

I noticed that anytime I come in with my car, it runs far away. Before, it never used to do that.

Now it runs and hides.

My daughter said maybe it has PTSD.

And honestly… I think animals do feel things like that.

I can imagine that kitten saw what happened. Saw its siblings die like that.

So now, whenever it sees a car, it is afraid.

That alone hurts in a different way.

I pray nobody ever has to experience something like this.

It is not a nice thing to go through.

But still… I accept it.

As a test.

From Allah.

Gimbia was the only survivor. Funke, Chioma, and Bodejo — forever remembered.

The Hajj Journey That Changed Me

March 28, 2025 • 10 min read

Faith & Spirituality Memoir

In 2019, I stood on the plains of Arafat surrounded by millions of souls from every corner of the earth. We spoke different languages, wore different clothes, carried different stories — but in that moment, we were the same. We were pilgrims seeking closeness to the Divine.

That journey became the seed for “An Epic Journey of Faith.” But more than a book, it became a lens through which I now see all my writing.

The Power of Shared Sacred Space

The Hajj taught me that the most powerful stories are those that remind us of our shared humanity. When I write — whether it's a poem about love or a novel about a prince seeking redemption — I'm always trying to create sacred space on the page.

“An Extraordinary Experience is exactly that. A faith-filled journey that touched my heart.” — Amazon Customer

Why I Write About Scars

March 10, 2025 • 4 min read

Poetry Inspiration

In my early years, at primary school in the 1980s, I stumbled upon an amazing poem about scars. Our teacher, bless her heart, explained how scars aren't just physical marks but reminders of the battles we've fought.

That lesson stuck with me. It inspired me to write two poetry collections and four novels. Because every character I create — Fantah, Hayah, Melody, Yusuf — carries scars. Not just the kind you can see, but the invisible ones we all bear.

Scars as Story

When I wrote Ripples, I thought about how every scar tells a story of survival. The collection became a meditation on becoming — moving from water to fire to simply being here.

“Ripples is a soul-stirring collection where each poem casts a stone into the still waters of memory.”

What's a scar — physical or emotional — that has shaped your story?

Bororo Girl to Published Author

February 20, 2025 • 7 min read

Culture & Identity Memoir

Fantah, the determined Bororo girl at the heart of my novel, shares more with me than readers might realize. Her journey — from the grasslands of Northern Nigeria to discovering her place in a complex world — mirrors my own.

Like Fantah, I grew up surrounded by traditions that felt both anchoring and limiting. I learned early that being a girl meant certain expectations. But I also learned, from the strong women around me, that expectations could be rewritten.

Finding My Voice in Multiple Languages

Today, I speak 3 languages fluently: English, Hausa, and Fulfulde. I can also read and write Arabic. Each language carries a different part of my identity. When I write, I'm not just choosing English because it's accessible — I'm translating entire worlds between the languages that shaped me.

“The compelling journey of Fantah takes readers on an unforgettable expedition through diverse landscapes and intriguing kingdoms.”

To every young girl from a small town with big dreams: your voice matters. Keep writing. Keep becoming.

February 5, 2025 • 9 min read

Behind the Books Fantasy

Yusuf's story began with a question: What if the space between sleep and wakefulness was actually a doorway?

I've always been fascinated by dreams — the way they can feel more real than waking life. In many Nigerian traditions, dreams are considered portals, messages from ancestors or glimpses into other dimensions. The Astral Series became my way of exploring that belief.

From Concept to Completion

Writing the Astral Series took me three years. I researched astral projection, traveled to learn from traditional spiritual guides, and spent countless nights journaling my own dreams. Yusuf's journey became my journey.

“Embark on a cosmic odyssey where boundaries between worlds dissolve. Join Yusuf as he discovers the ancient art of astral projection and unlocks the secrets of inter-dimensional travel.”

How to Find Your Voice as a Writer

April 2, 2025 • 6 min read

Writing Craft Inspiration

One question I receive often from aspiring writers is this: "How do I find my voice?"

It's a beautiful question because it shows you care about authenticity. You don't want to sound like anyone else. You want to sound like you.

After publishing seven books — poetry collections, novels, and everything in between — I've learned that finding your voice isn't about discovering something new. It's about uncovering what has always been there.

✦ Stop Trying to Sound Like Your Favorite Author

I love Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie. I love Toni Morrison. I love J.K. Rowling. But when I try to write like them, my words feel stiff. Fake. Borrowed.

Your favorite authors already exist. The world doesn't need another version of them. The world needs you.

Instead of asking, "How would Chimamanda write this?" ask, "How would I write this?" Your life, your experiences, your pain, your joy — no one else has lived what you have lived.

“Your voice is not something you create. It is something you remember.”

✦ Write Badly on Purpose

This sounds strange, doesn't it? But here's the truth: perfectionism kills voice.

When you're trying to write perfectly, you edit as you go. You delete sentences before they finish. You judge every word before it lands on the page.

Give yourself permission to write badly. Write a page of nonsense. Write a paragraph full of clichés. Write something so raw and unfiltered that you would never show it to anyone.

That mess? That's where your voice lives. Hidden beneath the need to be perfect.

✦ Read Your Own Words Aloud

Your voice isn't just about what you say — it's about how it sounds.

Read your writing aloud. Does it feel natural? Does it sound like something you would actually say to a friend? Or does it sound like a textbook or a formal essay?

When I write poetry, I read every line aloud. If my tongue trips over a word, I change it. If a sentence feels too long, I break it. Your ear knows your voice better than your eyes do.

✦ Write About What Only You Can Write About

Anyone can write about love. Anyone can write about grief. But only you can write about the specific way your grandmother laughed, or the smell of rain on the morning of your wedding, or the fear you felt on your first day of school.

As a Bororo girl growing up in Northern Nigeria, I write about cattle camps, Fulfulde proverbs, and the tension between tradition and modernity. These are mine. No one can take them.

What are yours? What memories, places, or people belong only to you? Start there.

✦ Stop Comparing Your First Draft to Someone Else's Published Book

This one hurts, but it's true.

That beautiful book you love? It went through dozens of revisions. An editor worked on it. A publishing team polished it.

Your messy first draft is supposed to be messy. That's not a sign that you lack voice. That's a sign that you're real.

Give yourself grace. Your voice will emerge through revision, not through perfection on the first try.

✦ Write Every Day (Even Just Five Minutes)

Voice is like a muscle. The more you use it, the stronger it becomes.

You don't need to write for hours. Five minutes a day is enough. Write about what you saw on your morning walk. Write about a conversation that stayed with you. Write about nothing at all.

Consistency matters more than quantity. Over time, patterns will emerge. You'll notice words you love, rhythms you return to, stories you can't stop telling.

That's your voice.

✦ Finally, Trust Yourself

After seven books, I still doubt my voice sometimes. I still wonder if I should write more like this author or that author.

But then I remember: the people who love my books don't love me because I sound like someone else. They love me because I sound like me.

Your voice is enough. It has always been enough. You just need to trust it.

✽

Start writing. Trust yourself. Your voice is waiting.

Now go write something only you can write. I can't wait to read it.

Why Do We Try to Break Each Other? — The Hidden Cost of Aggressive Bargaining in Nigeria

April 2, 2025 • 6 min read

Writing Craft Inspiration

📖 7 min read 🕊️ Reflection — fair trade & integrity 📅 April 7, 2026

There is something I have noticed—something quiet, but deeply rooted.

When many people in Nigeria want to buy something, they don’t just want a discount.

They want to win.

And sometimes, winning means breaking the person selling to them.

The Culture of “Pricing”

You go to a vendor.
You ask for the price.

They tell you.

And then it begins.

The negotiation.

Not just a small adjustment, not just a fair bargain—but a slow, persistent effort to bring the price down… and down… until it no longer makes sense.

Until the vendor is no longer making profit.

Sometimes, until they are making a loss.

And when it finally ends—when the vendor gives in—you can hear it.

That satisfaction.

That quiet pride.

As if something has been conquered.

But what exactly was won?

The Reality We Ignore

Yes, things are hard.

Everything is expensive.
Everyone is feeling the pressure.

But the vendor standing in front of you is not exempt from that reality.

They are also trying to survive.

They bought those goods from someone else.
They paid for transportation.
They paid for rent, utilities, and sometimes even advertising.

All of that before it even reaches you.

And yet, we stand there and keep pushing the price down, as if their effort means nothing.

Why?

How This Cycle Makes Everything Worse

Because of this behavior, something else begins to happen.

Vendors adapt.

If something should be sold for ₦12,000 or ₦14,000, they no longer say that.

They say ₦30,000.

Why?

Because they already expect the battle.

So when you negotiate it down to ₦18,000 or ₦20,000, you feel like you have won.

But in reality, you paid more than the original fair price.

And so the cycle continues.

Prices rise.
Trust disappears.
Life becomes harder—for everyone.

A Different Way (From Faith and Conscience)

As a Muslim, I reflect on what Islam teaches about trade.

Business is not meant to be a battlefield.

It is meant to be fair.

Both the buyer and the seller should leave satisfied.

Not cheated.
Not resentful.
Not wounded.

There is blessing in fairness.

When profit is honest—even if it is small—it carries barakah.
It stretches. It satisfies.

But money gained through pressure, deception, or harm?
It never truly benefits.

It comes with weight.

The Deeper Problem

This behavior is not limited to markets.

It appears everywhere.

In offices.
In systems.
In small everyday interactions.

Where something simple should take minutes, it takes weeks—unless something extra is given.

A bribe. A shortcut. A compromise.

And we wonder why things feel heavy.

Why nothing seems to move smoothly.

Why life feels like running in circles.

More Money, More Problems?

People say: “More money, more problems.”

But perhaps that is not entirely true.

Maybe it is not the money.

Maybe it is how the money was acquired.

When wealth is built on the discomfort of others, it carries consequences.

You cannot consistently take from people unfairly and expect peace in return.

A Simple Practice

There is something I have seen people do, especially in the North.

In the middle of negotiation, they pause and ask:

“Nawa gaskiya?”
“What is the true price?”

And then—whatever the answer is—they pay.

If the seller is honest, that money is blessed.

If the seller is dishonest, then the consequence is theirs to carry.

But you walk away with a clear heart.

A Question to Reflect On

Think back.

Someone who cheated you years ago—where are they now?

Did that gain truly elevate them?

Or did it simply pass through their hands, leaving something unsettled behind?

A Reminder

The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) emphasized honesty, fairness, and transparency in trade.

He taught that truthful and trustworthy merchants will be raised among the righteous.

And the Qur’an repeatedly warns against oppression—because to cheat someone is, in its own way, a form of oppression.

A Gentler Way Forward

Life is already difficult.

For everyone.

So why make it harder for each other?

Why not choose fairness?

Why not choose kindness?

Why not build relationships instead of small victories?

Let us be honest with each other.
Let us be considerate.
Let us remember that behind every price is a person trying to survive—just like you.

And maybe, if we change this one habit,
things will begin to feel a little lighter for all of us.

✨ Fairness is not weakness — it is barakah. ✨

The Recipe Google Couldn't Find

March 15, 2026 • 8 min read

Culture & Identity Food & Memory

How I became the first person to publish Peten Chinkafa online — and why some recipes only exist in grandmothers' kitchens until someone decides to write them down.

There is a particular kind of hunger that no restaurant can fix. It is the hunger for the food your grandmother made, in the kitchen that smelled of woodsmoke and palm oil, on a day when the harmattan wind made your bones ache.

For me, that food is Peten Chinkafa.

And until I wrote this post, it did not exist on the internet.

The Search That Led Nowhere

A few months ago, I had a craving. The kind that sits in your chest and refuses to leave. I wanted Peten Chinkafa — the watery, peppery, fish-rich rice porridge my mother made on cold mornings in Yola.

So I did what anyone would do. I opened Google.

I searched "Peten Chinkafa recipe." Nothing.

I searched "Fulani rice porridge." Nothing that matched.

I searched "Nigerian rice soup with garden eggs and fish." Close, but not it.

I tried every variation I could think of. I scrolled through Nigerian food blogs, YouTube cooking channels, Instagram pages. I found Peten Acha by the Jos people — a similar concept with acha grain instead of rice. But Peten Chinkafa? The specific dish I grew up eating?

Silence.

It was as if the internet had decided this food did not exist. As if my childhood, my grandmother's kitchen, the mornings I sat on a wooden stool watching her stir the pot — as if none of it was real enough to document.

That is when I realized: if I don't write this, no one will.

What Is Peten Chinkafa?

Peten Chinkafa is not fancy. It will not win plating competitions. It is a humble, watery rice dish — closer to porridge than pilau — made with short-grain rice, mixed dried fish (bonga and stock fish), garden eggs, peppers, and the distinctive tang of yakuwa, a sour spinach that Fulani cooks know by heart.

The name itself tells you what it is: Peten (porridge) Chinkafa (rice). Rice porridge.

But the simplicity is deceptive. The layers of flavor — the smokiness of the fish, the slow burn of attarugu, the slight bitterness of garden eggs balanced by yakuwa's sourness — create something that settles deep in your body. It is warmth in a bowl. It is the food my mother made when I had the flu. The food that made me feel safe.

The Comfort Food That Raised Me

I was born and raised in Yola, Adamawa State, in the heart of Northern Nigeria. My father was Nupe, from Niger State — a people with their own magnificent cuisine. But my mother was Fulani, and it was her kitchen that shaped my palate.

My grandmother's story is woven into this too. She was married to the chief of Madagali, a town in Adamawa known for its rich Fulani heritage. From her, my mother learned. From my mother, I learned. And now, from me, the internet will learn.

Growing up in Yola, my mornings often started with Fura and Nono — the thick, millet-based gruel paired with fermented cow's milk. I still crave it. There is nothing else like the cooling, sour tang of Nono cutting through the sweetness of fura on a hot afternoon.

But here is something many people don't know: we Fulani also eat our Nono with tuwon chinkafa — a thick rice pudding, distinct from the Hausa tuwo. It is unique to us. It is not written about. It is not photographed for food magazines. It is simply ours, passed quietly from mother to daughter, from kitchen to kitchen.

Peten Chinkafa exists in that same quiet space. It is everyday food. Comfort food. Healing food.

When I have the flu — even now, as an adult living in Abuja — I do not want pepper soup or medication. I want Peten Chinkafa. I want the steam rising from the bowl, the soft rice that requires no chewing, the fish that has given all its flavor to the broth. I want to feel like I am five years old again, wrapped in my mother's care.

"When I have the flu, I do not want pepper soup. I want Peten Chinkafa. I want to feel like I am five years old again, wrapped in my mother's care."

Why Fantah Exists Because of This Food

People often ask me what inspired my novel Fantah. They expect me to name a book, a film, a historical event.

But the truth is more personal.

Growing up in Yola, surrounded by Fulani culture — the language I speak fluently, the customs I understand instinctively, the food that formed my earliest memories — created a world inside me that demanded to be written. My grandmother's connection to Madagali. The cattle camps I saw as a child. The Bororo girls with their grace and resilience.

Fantah is Yola. Fantah is the Fulani girl I could have been, in a slightly different life. She carries the same strength I saw in the women around me. The same ability to make something nourishing from almost nothing.

Peten Chinkafa is part of that same story. It is what a Fulani mother makes when resources are thin but love is abundant. It is what I write about when I write about home.

The Responsibility of Being First

I did not want to be the first person to publish Peten Chinkafa online. I wanted someone else to have done it. I wanted to find a recipe, follow it, and feel connected to a larger tradition.

But that recipe did not exist. And so the responsibility fell to me.

This is what it means to be a writer, I think. Not just to tell stories, but to preserve them. To notice what is missing from the record and decide that your voice is the one that must fill the gap.

I wrote the recipe from memory, then called my mother to confirm. I described the texture — watery, like porridge, never dry — because that is the detail that matters. The detail that makes it Peten Chinkafa and not jollof rice with extra water.

I added the schema markup so Google understands what it is. I dated it May 2026 so the internet knows this was the moment it first appeared.

And now, when someone else searches — a Fulani girl in London missing her grandmother's kitchen, a food historian researching Nigerian porridges, a curious cook wanting to try something new — they will find it.

They will find me. And through me, they will find Yola. They will find my mother. They will find the chief of Madagali's wife, stirring her pot, passing down what no one thought to write down.

What I Want You to Take From This

If you are reading this and you also have a food that exists only in your memory — write it down. Do not wait for permission. Do not wait for someone more qualified.

You are qualified. You ate it. You survived on it. You loved it.

That is expertise enough.

The internet is vast, but it is not complete. There are still silences. Still empty search results. Still grandmothers' kitchens that exist only in the minds of those who sat in them.

Fill the silence. Write the recipe. Tell the story.

Peten Chinkafa is now alive online. And so, in a small but real way, is my grandmother's kitchen.
